Law of Triviality

Parkinson's law of triviality is C. Northcote Parkinson's 1957 argument that members of an organization give disproportionate weight to trivial issues, providing the example of a fictional committee whose job was to approve the plans for a nuclear power plant spending the majority of its time on discussions about relatively minor but easy-to-grasp issues, such as what materials to use for the staff bike shed, while neglecting the proposed design of the plant itself, which is far more important and a far more difficult and complex task.

For example...

Problem?

the city of Honolulu, HI had a very unpopular and fairly expensive website resource to help the citizens who rarely used it.

Solution!

  • city asks 3 volunteers to revamp site of 10,000+ pages
  • volunteers decide to make parallel site that better suits people’s expecations (looking for answers to questions.)
  • so they built honolulu answers; much more user friendly
  • team had to figure out how to generate so much content
  • so they crowdsourced the effort
  • in a single saturday afternoon
  • with local citizens, in a massive write-a-thon
Oakland, CA liked this so much they set about doing one, too (and succeeded.)

Problem?

Mexico City was about to spend the equivalent of 9.8 million dollars on a local legislation tracking application.

Solution!

  • local geeks, knowing this to be an outrageous contract, issued a challenge…
  • but it has to be completed within 10 days
  • though winners would be awarded 1/10000th as much ($9800) for their efforts
  • ultimately, they receive 173 submissions
  • 5 of which were presented to congress and are still in the app store today
  • this has since sparked a movement and saved the city a great deal of money

Problem?

The National Science Foundation was staring down the barrel of congressional budget cuts and the appearance that their research grants were not producing much socio-economic benefit.

Solution!

  • Code for NoVA (part of Code for America) took a look
  • they compared NSF data of awards granted in 2012 to PTO patents awarded since then
  • manually found that several grants had not been credited correctly with the patents awarded
  • wrote pattern matching routines to find more mislabelled patent awards
  • found 800% more assocations between grants and patents
  • spurred the PTO to improve their grant report form for patent filings
  • NOTE: Most of the work was done by high school students
